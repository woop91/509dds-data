# Learn about the Civil Service Commission appeals process

A summary of the various steps in the Civil Service Commission appeals process.

## First Step in the Appeals Process

The Civil Service Commission (CSC) appeals process starts when CSC receives an appeal.  The person filing the appeal is typically referred to as the "Appellant" and the appeal is typically filed against a government agency, referred to as the "Respondent".  CSC dockets the appeal by giving the appeal a docket number and emailing an appeal acknowledgement to both the Appellant and the Respondent, collectively known as "the parties".

In most cases, CSC will set up a remote pre-hearing conference **within 60 days** of receiving the appeal.

A representative or attorney can represent either party, but parties aren't required to have an attorney.  The Appellant is still required to attend the pre-hearing conference even if they are represented by an attorney.

Both parties and their counsel, if applicable, then receive a Webex videoconference email invitation from CSC which they should “accept”.  On the day of the pre-hearing conference, the parties will be using the “join meeting” button in that email to join the pre-hearing conference.  Webex email invitations may be forwarded to others by the parties if desired.

Any individual who plans on attending the remote pre-hearing conference and needs technical assistance with Webex should [visit the Webex test meeting](https://www.webex.com/test-meeting.html) site PRIOR to the pre-hearing.  If any person seeking to participate in the pre-hearing conference is having technical issues with Webex, they should contact CSC at [cschelp@mass.gov](mailto:cschelp@mass.gov) PRIOR to the pre-hearing.

Each party is required to submit a “Pre-Hearing Memorandum”, of no more than 1-4 pages, providing the following information:  a) a concise summary of their contentions; b) a list of prospective witnesses (note if subpoenas will be required); c) a list of proposed exhibits and/or anticipated requests for documents, discovery, etc; and d) acceptable dates for a potential full evidentiary hearing within 45 to 90 days of the pre-hearing (both parties should check with prospective witnesses regarding availability before the pre-hearing).

The pre-hearing memorandum should be submitted to [CSCdocuments@mass.gov](mailto:CSCdocuments@mass.gov)**at least one (1) week prior to the date of the pre-hearing conference**, with the opposing party copied on the email.

## What to expect at the Remote Pre-Hearing Conference

A pre-hearing conference is an informal meeting conducted remotely by a member of the Commission and typically lasts 30-40 minutes.

All participants in a remote pre-hearing conference must appear on a separate device, including counsel and his/her client, if applicable.

The Commissioner conducting the pre-hearing conference will cover the following topics:

- An overview of the civil service appeal process, including any questions that either party may have about the process.
- A reminder that all decisions issued by the Civil Service Commission are posted on the [Commission’s website](https://www.mass.gov/orgs/civil-service-commission).
- Agreement on undisputed facts related to the appeal, often called “stipulated facts”.
- If applicable, a discussion related to whether the Commission has jurisdiction to hear the appeal (i.e. – was it filed timely, etc.?).
- A preview of the argument and evidence (i.e. – documents, witness testimony) that each party will present at the potential next step of the process, which is usually a "full hearing", discussed in more detail below.
- If applicable, a discussion regarding the possibility of settling the matter without a full hearing.
- Any requests for information that either party may have of each other, to prepare for the full hearing.
- If necessary, agreement on a mutually acceptable date for a full hearing, which will typically be held within 45-90 days of the pre-hearing at the CSC offices in Boston or at an off-site hearing location in Springfield, Lowell or North Dartmouth. Before the pre-hearing, both parties **must** check their own availability and the availability of key witnesses so that a full hearing date can be set at the conclusion of the pre-hearing.

## How to Prepare for a Full Hearing

After the pre-hearing conference, certain appeals will be scheduled for what is called a "full hearing".  This full hearing is usually held **within 45-90 days** of the pre-hearing conference. The Chair assigns a Commissioner or Hearing Officer to hear the appeal.

Full hearings are typically held in-person in Boston, Springfield, Lowell or North Dartmouth.

Both parties will receive a Procedural Order and Notice of Full Hearing from the Commission which provides detailed information regarding how to prepare for the full hearing and who to call at CSC if they need assistance or have questions.  Below is an overview of what preparation is required.

It is important that both parties use the time before the full hearing wisely to ensure that they are prepared to present their case on the day of the full hearing. Both parties should use this time to complete the following:

1. Obtain any relevant documents that they wish to introduce at the full hearing.  If either party is requesting documents from the other party, they must put this request in writing.  If the other party does not produce the documents requested, the requesting party may ask the Commission, in writing, to “compel” the production of these documents. The initial request for documents, and, if necessary, the request to compel, must be completed before the full hearing.
2. If either party wishes to have a witness testify on the day of the full hearing, they may ask this person to appear voluntarily or, if necessary, may [request authorization from the Commission to issue a subpoena](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/request-a-subpoena-for-a-civil-service-commission-hearing) to require this person’s attendance at the hearing.

**The following are required to be submitted at least two weeks prior to the full hearing unless a different deadline was established in a procedural order sent to the parties:**

Please submit the following information to the Commission, either to the hearing officer or [CSCdocuments@mass.gov](mailto:CSCdocuments@mass.gov), with a copy to the opposing party:

1. An electronic copy of exhibits in PDF format.  It is important to follow the instructions that you received after the pre-hearing conference regarding the format of the exhibits, including the need to:a) [redact all personal identifying information](https://www.mass.gov/doc/civil-service-commission-hearing-guidelines-to-protect-confidentiality/download); b) separate exhibits with a title page that includes the number and title of the exhibit; c) insert bates numbers on the top left corner of the exhibits.
2. An electronic copy of a separate exhibit list in Excel format.  Please use the spreadsheet that was forwarded to you after the pre-hearing conference.
3. An electronic copy of a separate participant list in Excel format.  Please use the spreadsheet that was forwarded to you after the pre-hearing conference.

**The following are required to be submitted on the day of the full hearing****:**

On the day of the full hearing, each party is required to bring the following:

1. **Four** hard copies of the exhibits for:  a) you; b) the opposing party; c) witnesses; d) hearing officer.
2. **Three** hard copies of the Exhibit List for:  a) you; b) the opposing party; and c) hearing officer.

**Other important items for your hearing:**

Prepare a brief “opening statement” to give at the beginning of the full hearing. This should be a brief preview of your case (i.e. – what the hearing officer should expect to see in the documents presented or hear from the witnesses). These opening statements are typically no more than 5 minutes.

When possible, meet with the other party to reach agreement on the documents to be submitted and any other stipulated facts which may minimize the number of witnesses who will need to testify.

## Requesting a continuance

### Pre-Hearing

For pre-hearings only, the Commission allows a one-time continuance for any reason, if the following requirements are met:

- The party requesting the continuance contacts the other party and gets mutually acceptable dates for a re-scheduled pre-hearing to be held within 30 days.
- The party requesting the continuance submits the request to the Commission’s Office Manager, copied to the other party, with the mutually acceptable new date to the Commission’s Office Manager.

You can submit continuance requests via email to [cscdocuments@mass.gov](mailto:cscdocuments@mass.gov).

### Full Hearing

For full hearings, the CSC only grants continuances in extraordinary circumstances and must be approved by the Chair of the Commission or the Commissioner who has been assigned to conduct the full hearing. Ongoing settlement discussions and/or appearance by counsel in another forum are not considered extraordinary circumstances.

## What to Expect at a Full Hearing

A full hearing is a formal proceeding conducted by a member of the Commission, who will serve as the hearing officer. It is important that both parties and their witnesses appear on time at the hearing location listed on the full hearing notice.  For disciplinary appeals, the hearing is private (closed to the public) unless either party makes a request for a public hearing.  All other hearings (i.e. – bypass appeals, classification, examination appeals, etc.) are open to the public.

01. If the hearing is in Boston, all hearing participants should check-in at the security desk on the second floor of the Saltonstall building with a valid government ID and proceed to the assigned Court Room on the same floor.  For hearings conducted off-site, hearing participants should follow the directions on the notice of full hearing.
02. If either party has witnesses testifying on their behalf, they greet the witnesses, ask them to have a seat, and let them know that they will be informed when their testimony is required.
03. The hearing officer will call the case and ask both parties to have a seat at the hearing table in the hearing room.
04. Prior to going on the record (turning on the recorder), the hearing officer may opt to complete certain tasks “off the record”.  For example, the hearing officer may choose to review the proposed documents off the record and note any objections after the formal proceeding has begun.
05. The hearing officer will officially open the full hearing by reading an introduction, which includes references to the [rules under which the hearing is conducted](https://www.mass.gov/regulations/801-CMR-100-standard-adjudicatory-rules-of-practice-and-procedure), and a reminder that all Commission decisions are posted on the [Commission’s website](https://www.mass.gov/orgs/civil-service-commission).
06. Each party will then be given an opportunity to make an opening statement.
07. Typically, the Respondent (i.e. – the Appointing Authority) will present their case first, by calling their witnesses and asking them questions.  This is referred to as “direct testimony”.
08. After direct testimony is completed, the Appellant has the opportunity to ask these witnesses questions. This is referred to as “cross examination.”
09. Throughout the hearing, the hearing officer will make rulings, if necessary, regarding any objections to taking certain testimonies (i.e. – is it relevant?). The Commission is not bound by the Court’s Rules of Evidence and, in some cases, may allow hearsay testimony if it meets certain criteria.
10. After each of the witnesses for the Respondent has testified, the Respondent will then “rest” their case.
11. At this point, the Appellant will have the opportunity to present witnesses, ask the witnesses questions (direct testimony), and subject them to cross examination by the Respondent. The Appellant will also have the opportunity to testify on his or her own behalf.
12. At the conclusion of the testimony of the Appellant and his or her witnesses, the Appellant will then “rest” his / her case.
13. The hearing officer will then discuss with the parties whether he/she will conclude the hearing through closing statements or, in some cases, whether the parties will have the opportunity to submit what are referred to as “proposed decisions” to the hearing officer, typically within 30 days of the close of the full hearing.
14. If proposed decisions are accepted, the hearing officer will provide both parties with instructions on how to complete / submit these proposed decisions.
15. Within 2-3 days of the full hearing, both parties will receive a recording of the hearing electronically.

## What Happens After the Full Hearing

After the full hearing, the parties may, at the discretion of the hearing officer, be asked to submit “proposed decisions”, typically within 30 days of completing the full hearing.

The hearing officer is responsible for drafting a decision to be reviewed by the full Civil Service Commission as part of a bi-weekly Executive Session.

After the Commission votes on the decision, the final decision is emailed to both parties to the email addresses listed on the appearance form. The Commission does **not** send an additional copy of the decision via first class mail.

The Commission has established targets regarding the time it takes to process an appeal from appeal file date to the issuance of the decision.  Current targets and statistics regarding cycle time for processing appeals can be found at:  [Civil Service Commission Statistics](https://www.mass.gov/lists/civil-service-commission-statistics).

## The Right to Appeal a Commission Decision or Order

Any final decision or order issued by the Commission includes information about the right to file a:

- Motion for reconsideration with the Commission
- Judicial appeal of the decision with the Superior Court

Either party may file a motion for reconsideration within ten days of receipt of a Commission order or decision. Under the pertinent provisions of the Code of Mass. Regulations, 801 C.M.R. § 1.01(7)(l), the motion must identify a clerical or mechanical error in this order or decision or a significant factor the Agency or the Presiding Officer may have overlooked in deciding the case.  A motion for reconsideration **does not** toll the statutorily prescribed thirty-day time limit for seeking judicial review of this Commission order or decision.

Under the provisions of G.L c. 31, § 44, any party aggrieved by a Commission order or decision may initiate proceedings for judicial review under G.L. c. 30A, § 14 in the superior court within thirty (30) days after receipt of the order or decision. Commencement of such proceeding shall not, unless specifically ordered by the court, operate as a stay of the Commission order or decision.  After initiating proceedings for judicial review in Superior Court, the plaintiff, or his / her attorney, is required to serve a copy of the summons and complaint upon the Boston office of the Attorney General of the Commonwealth, with a copy to the Civil Service Commission, in the time and in the manner prescribed by Mass. R. Civ. P. 4(d).
